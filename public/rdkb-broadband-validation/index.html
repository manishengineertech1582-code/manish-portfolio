RDK-B Platform Overview

Prepared for engineering, architecture, product, and industry review
RDK-B validation and diagnostics platform

Built to turn scattered broadband evidence into standards-grounded root-cause decisions.

This platform ingests Wi-Fi, network, self-heal, telemetry, and management-plane data, correlates signals across RDK-B subsystems, and produces actionable findings with confidence, evidence lineage, and recommended fixes. It is designed for engineering validation, release certification, field triage, and cross-team operational review.

What the system does
  • Analyzes RDK-B logs including Wi-Fi, hostapd, dnsmasq, SelfHeal, and telemetry streams.
  • Correlates those signals with WebPA, SNMP, and TR-181 state rather than diagnosing each interface in isolation.
  • Detects steering loops, reconnect churn, DHCP instability, poor client experience, and wired link problems.
  • Produces root cause, validation state, confidence, and a concrete remediation path for each incident window.
Why it matters
  • Manual debugging across broadband subsystems is slow and error-prone.
  • Pure rule engines overfire on symptoms and miss cross-layer causes.
  • Field and lab teams need findings they can trace back to logs and standards.
  • Product leaders need faster release confidence with fewer hidden regressions.

Problem Statement

RDK-B validation rarely fails cleanly in one place. A customer experience issue might start as unstable RSSI, surface as repeated disassociation in hostapd, later appear as DHCP churn in dnsmasq, and finally trigger recovery behavior in SelfHeal. Teams that review only one subsystem often fix the symptom, not the cause. The platform addresses this by grounding every diagnosis in correlated evidence across logs, telemetry, and device-management views.

Manual validation gaps
  • Time spent stitching timestamps and device states by hand
  • Inconsistent interpretation across test teams
  • No durable evidence chain for leadership review
Rule-only limitations
  • Threshold alerts that do not explain sequence or causality
  • Poor handling of silent or partial failures
  • Weak cross-interface reasoning across TR-181, WebPA, and logs
Platform objective
  • Surface the highest-confidence root cause first
  • Preserve source attribution and standards grounding
  • Turn validation output into action, not just alert volume

System Overview and Detailed Architecture

The platform follows a layered architecture so that evidence collection, feature derivation, deterministic validation, and AI reasoning stay independently auditable. This keeps the system explainable and helps ensure that generated narrative never outranks actual device evidence.

1
Ingest

Collect Wi-Fi, hostapd, dnsmasq, SelfHeal, telemetry, WebPA, TR-181, and SNMP inputs for the test window.

2
Normalize

Standardize timestamps, device identifiers, client MAC references, and subsystem event structure.

3
Correlate

Group related events into incident windows and link management-plane state with runtime evidence.

4
Validate

Apply deterministic checks for known failure patterns, standards alignment, and cross-source consistency.

5
Explain

Generate grounded diagnosis, confidence, and remediation only from the validated evidence set.

Data layer

Responsible for ingestion, parsing, normalization, time alignment, and structured feature staging across RDK-B, telemetry, and control-plane sources.

Intelligence layer

Handles correlation, event sequencing, deterministic rules, evidence ranking, and grounded AI reasoning over the validated incident context.

Validation layer

Produces PASS, FAIL, or PARTIAL state, root-cause narrative, citations, confidence, and recommended next actions for lab and field workflows.

AI and Validation Approach

The platform is intentionally hybrid. Deterministic checks establish whether a known failure signature exists. AI reasoning then explains what happened, why the evidence points to that conclusion, and what should be done next. This preserves explainability while still giving teams natural-language insight and cross-system summarization.

Deterministic validation first
  • Rules identify explicit failure signatures such as RSSI steering loops, repeated deauth, DHCP churn, and Ethernet flaps.
  • Cross-source checks decide whether a symptom is primary or downstream of another event chain.
  • Evidence from logs and management state forms the gating input to reasoning.
Grounded AI reasoning second
  • Explains findings in engineer-readable language without inventing unsupported causes.
  • Uses prompt engineering to rank evidence, preserve uncertainty, and keep standards citations visible.
  • Supports zero-shot reasoning for explanation and few-shot shaping only where response structure needs consistency.
Training in this system

Training means prompt tuning, domain knowledge injection, labeled failure pattern refinement, and ongoing pattern library expansion rather than unconstrained model guessing.

No-hallucination posture

Missing evidence stays missing. Confidence drops when corroboration is weak. The narrative must point back to actual logs, telemetry, or standards references.

Failure detection intelligence

The engine explicitly looks for silent failures, partial failures, and configuration-state mismatches that do not show up as a single hard outage.

Standards and Compliance Alignment

The validation model is tied to broadband and device-management standards so that test verdicts and recommended fixes remain defensible across engineering, certification, and partner review.

Precedence 1
IEEE standards for Wi-Fi, Ethernet, PHY, MAC, and radio behavior.
Precedence 2
RDK-B specifications, component behavior, log semantics, and platform implementation rules.
Precedence 3
TR-181, TR-069, TR-369, SNMP RFC guidance, DOCSIS context, and established industry best practices.
Wi-Fi and Ethernet

IEEE 802.11 and Ethernet standards anchor PHY, MAC, steering, roaming, link quality, and wired fault interpretation.

Device management

TR-181 and TR-069 or TR-369 guide object-level truth, remote configuration, and control-plane state comparison.

Operational validation

SNMP and DOCSIS references help correlate counters, WAN behavior, and fleet-facing operations with device logs.

Key Use Cases

The platform is built for validation teams, broadband architects, and support operations that need more than an alarm list. It prioritizes repeatable scenarios that directly affect customer experience and release confidence.

Wi-Fi steering and roaming

Diagnose RSSI-triggered steering loops, sticky-client behavior, failed BSS transitions, and client roaming degradation.

Service instability

Separate DHCP and DNS symptoms from upstream Wi-Fi or management-plane causes before they become misclassified defects.

Performance and release validation

Use telemetry and logs together to validate firmware behavior, QoE trends, security posture, and operational regressions.

Representative Incident Walkthrough

A typical scenario starts with poor signal and unstable steering behavior, then expands into reconnect churn and DHCP noise. The system treats those events as a chain, not as unrelated alerts.

Detection

Wi-Fi logs show repeated low-RSSI steering decisions and elevated retry behavior for the same client device.

Correlation

hostapd shows repeated association or deauthentication churn, while telemetry records rising disconnect count in the same incident window.

Cross-system validation

dnsmasq records address renegotiation attempts, but management-plane checks confirm the DHCP subsystem itself is configured and reachable.

Root cause decision

The platform ranks Wi-Fi instability as the primary cause and DHCP churn as the downstream symptom, then recommends steering threshold and channel policy review.

Advantages, Limitations, and Forward Roadmap

Compared with manual debugging, the system cuts triage time and preserves evidence lineage. Compared with threshold-only rule engines, it provides cross-layer diagnosis and grounded narrative. Its current limitation is simple: it can only be as strong as the evidence available. Missing logs, time skew, vendor-specific format drift, or weak control-plane visibility reduce confidence and must remain explicit in the output.

Why it is better
  • Faster root-cause isolation
  • Cross-source validation
  • Engineer-readable findings tied to standards
Current limitations
  • Dependent on source coverage and timestamp quality
  • Needs continued expansion for new vendor log variants
  • Confidence must drop when evidence is incomplete
Future enhancements
  • Autonomous remediation with approval gates
  • Closed-loop revalidation after corrective actions
  • ML-based anomaly detection for emerging patterns
